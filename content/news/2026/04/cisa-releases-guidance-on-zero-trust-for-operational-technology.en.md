Joint guidance from CISA, DoE, FBI, and others helps OT owners apply zero trust principles to address IT-OT convergence risks.

{{< cyber-report severity="Info" source="CISA" target="Operational Technology (OT) systems" >}}

CISA, in coordination with the Department of War, Department of Energy, Federal Bureau of Investigation, and Department of State, has released a joint guidance document titled 'Adapting Zero Trust Principles to Operational Technology.' The guidance aims to help organizations apply zero trust (ZT) principles to operational technology (OT) environments, which are increasingly interconnected with IT systems.

{{< ad-banner >}}

Zero trust eliminates implicit trust and requires continuous validation of access based on identity, context, and risk. The convergence of IT and OT introduces new cybersecurity risks, making perimeter-based defenses inadequate. The guidance addresses challenges such as legacy infrastructure, operational constraints, and safety requirements, emphasizing asset visibility, supply chain risk management, and robust identity and access management.

Key recommendations include implementing layered security measures like network segmentation, secure communication protocols, and vulnerability management. This guidance supports OT owners and operators in transitioning to a zero trust architecture while considering the unique constraints of OT systems.

{{< netrunner-insight >}}

For SOC analysts and DevSecOps engineers, this guidance underscores the need to extend zero trust principles beyond traditional IT into OT environments. Practical takeaways include prioritizing asset discovery and inventory for OT devices, implementing network segmentation to isolate critical processes, and enforcing strict identity-based access controls even for legacy systems. The convergence of IT and OT means that traditional perimeter defenses are no longer sufficient; continuous monitoring and validation are essential.
