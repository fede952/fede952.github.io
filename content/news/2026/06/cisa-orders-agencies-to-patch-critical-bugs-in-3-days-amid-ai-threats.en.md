CISA warns defenders cannot afford weeks to patch, issuing a directive requiring US agencies to fix security vulnerabilities in as little as three days due to AI-driven threats.

{{< cyber-report severity="High" source="Wired Security" target="US federal agencies" >}}

The Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) has issued a new directive requiring US federal agencies to patch security vulnerabilities within as little as three days, citing the increased speed and sophistication of AI-powered cyberattacks. The directive reflects a significant acceleration of typical patching timelines, which often span weeks or months.

{{< ad-banner >}}

A CISA official warned on Wednesday that 'defenders cannot afford to take weeks to patch,' emphasizing the urgency imposed by adversaries leveraging artificial intelligence to automate and expedite exploitation of known vulnerabilities. The directive applies to all federal civilian executive branch agencies and is expected to influence private sector best practices.

While no specific CVEs or CVSS scores were mentioned in the announcement, the directive underscores a broader trend toward faster remediation cycles. Organizations are advised to prioritize asset inventory, automate patch deployment, and maintain robust vulnerability management programs to comply with the shortened timelines.
