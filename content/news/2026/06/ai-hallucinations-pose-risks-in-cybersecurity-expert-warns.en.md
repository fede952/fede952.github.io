Enrico Frumento of Cefriel warns that AI's inability to admit ignorance can lead to dangerous hallucinations in critical cybersecurity contexts, potentially causing severe damage.

{{< cyber-report severity="Info" source="Cybersecurity360" target="AI systems in cybersecurity" >}}

Artificial intelligences suffer from hallucinations because they cannot perceive the limits of their knowledge and are trained never to respond 'I don't know'. This inherent flaw becomes particularly dangerous when applied to critical fields such as cybersecurity, where incorrect outputs can have devastating consequences.

{{< ad-banner >}}

Enrico Frumento, Cybersecurity Research Lead at Cefriel, highlights that the lack of self-awareness in AI systems leads to confident yet erroneous responses. In security operations, such hallucinations could mislead analysts, trigger false alarms, or overlook genuine threats, amplifying risks rather than mitigating them.

The article underscores the need for human oversight and robust validation mechanisms when deploying AI in security contexts. As AI adoption grows, understanding and mitigating these limitations is crucial to prevent AI from becoming a liability instead of an asset.

{{< netrunner-insight >}}

SOC analysts should never fully trust AI-generated alerts without cross-referencing raw data. DevSecOps teams must implement strict validation pipelines and consider 'I don't know' as a valid AI response to reduce hallucination risks. Treat AI as a junior analyst—useful but requiring supervision.
