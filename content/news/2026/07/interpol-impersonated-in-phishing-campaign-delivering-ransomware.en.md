A phishing campaign uses fake Interpol emails with legal jargon to trick victims into opening malicious attachments that deploy ransomware. Learn how to defend.

{{< cyber-report severity="High" source="Cybersecurity360" target="Email users" >}}

A new phishing campaign has been identified where attackers send fraudulent emails impersonating official Interpol communications. The emails use formal language and legal references to appear legitimate, convincing recipients to open malicious attachments that contain ransomware.

{{< ad-banner >}}

The campaign exploits trust in international law enforcement to lower victims' defenses. Once the attachment is opened, the ransomware encrypts files and demands payment. No specific CVE or CVSS score is mentioned in the report.

Organizations should educate users to verify unexpected emails from authorities, avoid opening attachments from unverified sources, and maintain offline backups. Implementing email filtering and multi-factor authentication can also reduce risk.

{{< netrunner-insight >}}

SOC analysts should monitor for emails with Interpol branding and legal language, as these are red flags. DevSecOps teams should ensure email gateways block attachments from unknown senders and that endpoint detection can catch ransomware behavior early.
